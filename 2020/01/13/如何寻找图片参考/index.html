如何寻找图片参考

PART I

It’s been a long time since something’s been written here… but today is the day. 

Photo-references - that should the first thing on your list when starting a new arch-viz project. Let’s talk about ways we can find and organise them.

photo by Taiyo Watanabe

To be precise, that’s something that you should be looking out for at any spare moment and literary everywhere - see a mesmerising sunset sky, take out your phone and snap a photo, see a beautifully lit building, well, snap a photo. Same thing with internet - see something that catches your attention, let’s say on ArchDaily - screenshot it or save it. 

After a while you’ll start building an impressive library with many moods, times of the day, colours, the most important part here is to get organised.

I tend to store all my references in MacOS native Photos app, but there are many alternatives for both Windows & Mac like Adobe Lightroom, Google Photos and many others, you just have to find one for you liking.

Good start is to split images into 2 categories - INTERIOR & EXTERIOR, Then go deeper and organise them (exterior) by time of the day/lighting conditions such as - sunset/sunrise, dusk/dawn, midday sunny, overcast, night etc.

Interior, on the other hand, can be organised by its type - residential, corporate, public etc.

The goal here is to keep it simple, but at the same time manageable, so once you have specific image in mind you can quickly find a suitable reference.

PART II

Secondly, I made a classic rookie mistake, this post has been long overdue, but, THANK GOD I outsmarted myself naming previous post - PART 1.

So, here it comes - PHOTO REFERENCES PART 2 or how to use gathered photo-references to start producing actual work.

So, where should you start?

It all starts with figuring out type of mood you are after in your image - from dusk till dawn as you may have heard.

As architects and developers more and more rely on selling their projects and products using renderings we produce, unfortunately (or fortunately), most demanding images are the ones that are clean, happy and sunny, which assumably makes it a ‘safe’ bet in the long run. It does make it ‘safe’ though, but on the flip-side it also makes it average and predictable, it is pretty hard to stand out with images that has all the familiar clichés in them like happy, laughing people, clean sky with birds, kids with balloons etc… you get the point.

So, in order to stand out and try to make our work as interesting as possible, safe bet would be everything except ‘safe’ bet. In other words all the moods except midday sunny, happy, laughing with balloons and birds.

Before I proceed though, let me clarify - there is nothing wrong with midday sun images. I’ve created them before and will continue to do so in the future, it just takes a bit more ‘out of box’ thinking when creating that type of images - it could be eye catching frame composition, direction of light, amount and placement of shadows or as simple as simplicity of elements in it. Ok, back at it, as I said earlier, it all starts with figuring out type of mood you are going to use (of course if it’s not already predefined) - what comes handy here is to ask yourself (or client) a couple of key questions:

  • What type of building is that (shelter, single family house, residential building, school, hospital, stadium)?- Where is it located (describe surrounding landscape - downtown, uptown, fields, mountains, forest)?- What emotion should it convey?
    Next up make a note on usual suspects - shape of building, materiality (colour palette of building), camera angles that might work (composition). At this point you should have somewhat clear understanding where is it heading - in other words, in which library you should start looking - sunset/sunrise, dusk/dawn, midday sunny, overcast, rainy, night etc.

Once you are certain regarding what mood/s you are after you can finally start looking in your reference library and see what matches building, environment and composition (look for similarly shaped buildings, similar colour pallets and compositions, or simply images that you think could work here and pull all of them into .PSD file). Be aware that at the end it will come down to 1-3 images that will be used, while most of them will be discarded in the process anyways and that’s normal, thats part of it.

To give you a better understanding, here is how I did it for attic cottages project:

Key questions?

  • It’s a cabin. - Located in wild and harsh landscape of Terrence Bay forest. - Should feel secure, warm (it is intended to be used as a temporary shelter for hikers, bikers, rock climbers, kayakers etc.).

    What info I had?

I had full colour palette of building, which in this case was simple - concrete base, black steel facade, plywood interior - somewhat predefined viewpoints and basic understanding of composition.

Decision?

Considering all info above, there were 2 types of moods I was after - clear autumn sunrise or cloudy overcast/cloudy sunrise (below). Since I wanted to showcase contrast between warm interior and harsh environment, I went for overcast.

Once you are set on the reference/s, rest is just matching it as close as possible (match lighting and light direction, contrast between sky and ground, tonality of vegetation and building materials) from 1-3 references (the more references you use, the bigger chance of chaotic failure at the end). Train your eye for it - some personal projects might help here, since you are just trying to recreate a photograph.

That’s all folks, in PART 3 I will focus on finishing touches, which means - postproduction. While in the development stage (modeling, lighting, texturing) we nurture it into right direction, postproduction is the place where magic and fine tuning happens.
